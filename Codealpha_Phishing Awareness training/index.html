MODULE 01

What is Phishing?

🎣
Phishing is a type of cyberattack where criminals disguise themselves as trusted entities — banks, employers, government agencies — to trick people into revealing sensitive information such as passwords, credit card numbers, or personal identity data. The name derives from "fishing" — baiting victims with convincing lures.

⚙️ How a Phishing Attack Works

1
Attacker crafts a convincing message impersonating a trusted brand or person
2
Message is sent to the victim with urgent calls to action or enticing offers
3
Victim clicks a malicious link or opens a harmful attachment
4
Attacker steals credentials, installs malware, or commits financial fraud

🧠 Why It's So Effective

  • Exploits human psychology, not just technical flaws
  • Uses urgency and fear to bypass rational thinking
  • Extremely low cost for attackers, massive ROI
  • Requires no advanced hacking — only deception
  • Can bypass even sophisticated security systems
  • AI makes it more convincing and personalized than ever
  • Constantly evolves to evade detection filters

📅 Evolution of Phishing

1990s
First phishing attacks targeted AOL dial-up users, stealing account passwords via fake chat messages
2000s
Attacks expanded to financial institutions — PayPal, eBay, banks — mass email campaigns began
2010s
Spear phishing emerged with targeted, personalized attacks using OSINT from social media
2020s
AI-generated phishing, deepfake voice scams, and real-time phishing kits dominate the threat landscape
MODULE 02

Types of Phishing Attacks

Know your enemy — eight common phishing attack styles to recognize

📧 HIGH RISK
Email Phishing

The most common form — mass emails impersonating well-known brands (PayPal, Microsoft, Amazon) to steal login credentials or financial data.

🎯 CRITICAL
Spear Phishing

Highly targeted attacks using personal details gathered from LinkedIn, social media, or previous data breaches to appear completely convincing.

🐳 CRITICAL
Whaling

Spear phishing directed specifically at C-suite executives (CEO, CFO, CTO) to gain corporate access or authorize large financial transactions.

📱 HIGH RISK
Smishing

Phishing via SMS messages — fake delivery alerts, bank OTP requests, prize notifications designed to extract info or install malware.

📞 MEDIUM
Vishing

Voice phishing — attackers call posing as tech support, IRS agents, or bank security teams to verbally extract sensitive information.

🔄 HIGH RISK
Clone Phishing

A legitimate email previously received is cloned and re-sent with malicious attachments or links replacing the originals.

🌐 HIGH RISK
Pharming

DNS manipulation redirects legitimate website traffic to fraudulent clones — victims are deceived even when typing the correct URL.

🤖 CRITICAL
AI Phishing

Next-generation attacks using LLMs to craft grammatically perfect, highly personalized phishing content at massive scale — the emerging frontier threat.

Recognizing Phishing Emails

Click the red flags below to learn what to watch for

📧 INTERACTIVE EMAIL — Tap the ⚑ flags to reveal the suspicious details
From: security@paypa1-account-alert.com
🚨 Spoofed Sender Domain
Notice "paypa1" — the letter L is replaced with the number 1. Real PayPal emails only ever come from @paypal.com. This subtle substitution tricks your eye at a glance.
Subject: ‼️ URGENT: Your account has been COMPROMISED — Verify NOW or lose access in 24hrs!
🚨 Urgency & Fear Tactics
Words like "URGENT", "COMPROMISED", "NOW", "24hrs" are engineered to trigger panic, bypassing your critical thinking. Legitimate companies use calm, professional language in their communications.

Dear Valued Customer,

🚨 Generic Salutation
PayPal knows your name — they'd write "Dear John Smith." Generic greetings like "Dear Customer" or "Dear User" reveal this was sent to thousands of random victims.

We have detected multiple unauthorized login attempts on your PayPal account. Your account will be permanently disabled in 24 hours unless you immediately verify your identity.

Please click the secure button below to restore your account access and prevent permanent data loss.

🚨 Credential Harvesting Attempt
Legitimate organizations NEVER ask you to click email links to verify credentials. Always navigate directly to the website by typing the official URL in your browser.

🔒 VERIFY MY ACCOUNT IMMEDIATELY
🚨 Malicious Hyperlink
Hovering this button would reveal the real destination: http://paypal-verify.russianhackers.ru/steal-login — a completely different domain. Always check where a link actually goes before clicking.

This is an automated mesage. Plese do not reply. If you have any questons contact are customer support team at PayPal.

🚨 Grammar & Spelling Errors
"mesage", "Plese", "questons", "are customer" — multiple errors expose this as a scam. Real PayPal has professional copywriters and editors. Poor language quality is a reliable red flag.

📧
Suspicious Sender

⏰

"Act NOW", "24 hours", "account will be deleted" — designed to short-circuit rational thinking
👤

MODULE 04

Spotting Fake Websites

✓ LEGITIMATE
https:// www.paypal.com /login
  • ✓ HTTPS with valid SSL certificate
  • ✓ Correct domain name spelling
  • ✓ Trusted TLD (.com, official)
  • ✓ No extra subdomains or words
VS
⚠ PHISHING
http:// paypal-verify-secure .suspicious-login.ru/confirm
  • ✗ No HTTPS — connection is insecure
  • ✗ "PayPal" is a subdomain — not the real domain
  • ✗ Foreign TLD (.ru) — Russia, not a company
  • ✗ Extra words to create false trust
🔒
HTTPS ≠ Safety

HTTPS only means your connection is encrypted — NOT that the site is legitimate. Phishing sites widely use HTTPS. Always check the domain name, not just the lock icon.

🌐
Read the Full URL

The actual domain is the part right before the first standalone slash. amazon.com.evil-login.net — the real domain is evil-login.net, not Amazon.

🎨
Visual Quality

Blurry logos, broken layouts, mixed fonts, or slightly off color schemes can indicate a cloned site. However, sophisticated phishing sites can look pixel-perfect.

📜
Check Legal Pages

Fake sites often have missing, generic, or copy-pasted Privacy Policies and Terms. No contact information or a fake address is also a red flag.

🔍
Navigate Directly

Never click email links to visit your bank or PayPal. Open a new tab, type the address yourself, or use your bookmarks. This single habit prevents most phishing attacks.

📅
Check Domain Age

Use who.is or whois.domaintools.com to see when a domain was registered. Phishing sites are often days old. Legitimate businesses have years-old domains.

MODULE 05

Social Engineering Tactics

The psychological tricks attackers use to manipulate human behavior

👑
Authority

Impersonating figures of authority — CEO, IT department, government, law enforcement — to compel compliance without questioning.

Real Example: "This is Microsoft Support. Your computer is broadcasting a virus signal. You must install our diagnostic tool immediately."
⏱️
Urgency & Scarcity

Artificial time pressure prevents you from stopping to verify. When you feel rushed, your brain skips critical evaluation.

Real Example: "Your account will be permanently deleted in 2 hours. This is your FINAL WARNING."
😨
Fear & Intimidation

Threatening legal action, arrest, or catastrophic consequences to activate a fear response that overrides logical thinking.

Real Example: "IRS FINAL NOTICE: You owe $3,847. Law enforcement has been notified. Call immediately to avoid arrest."
🤝
Familiarity & Trust

Using your name, job title, recent projects, or colleague names gathered from OSINT to appear as someone you know and trust.

Real Example: "Hi Sarah! John from Marketing here. Can you send me the Q3 client list? Working from home today."
🎁
Reciprocity & Reward

Offering something valuable — gift cards, prizes, refunds — in exchange for your information, exploiting our natural tendency to give back.

Real Example: "Congratulations! You've won a $500 Amazon gift card. Verify your details within 24 hours to claim!"
👥
Social Proof

Fabricating popularity or consensus to make malicious actions appear normal, safe, and widely accepted.

Real Example: "2.4 million users have already verified their accounts. Update your security settings now to stay protected."
🧠

The Defense Mindset

When you feel pressured, excited, or afraid — that's exactly when to slow down. Attackers engineer emotional states to override your logic. The moment you feel urgency, treat it as a red flag. Pause. Verify. Then act.

MODULE 06

Best Practices & Defense Guide

01
Never Click Suspicious Links

Hover to preview the actual destination URL before clicking. Navigate directly by typing official URLs. Use virustotal.com to scan suspicious links without visiting them.

02
Enable Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)

Even if your password is stolen, MFA requires a second factor — TOTP code, hardware key, or biometric — making unauthorized access exponentially harder. Enable it everywhere.

03
Verify Through Official Channels

If an email requests urgent action, call the organization using the phone number from their official website — not the number provided in the suspicious email.

04
Keep All Software Updated

Enable automatic updates for your OS, browsers, and antivirus. Many phishing attacks exploit known vulnerabilities in outdated software that patches would have fixed.

05
Use a Password Manager

Password managers autofill credentials only on legitimate domains — they won't fill your PayPal password on paypa1-alert.com. They also encourage unique passwords for every account.

06
Report Every Phishing Attempt

Report to your IT/security team, your email provider (Gmail, Outlook both have "Report Phishing" buttons), and official bodies like the FBI IC3 (ic3.gov) or APWG (reportphishing.apwg.org).

07
Limit Your Digital Footprint

Attackers use LinkedIn, Facebook, and company directories for spear phishing. Audit your privacy settings and be selective about what personal and professional information you share publicly.

08
Think Before You Click — Always

The golden rule: pause and question every unexpected email. Were you expecting this? Does it make sense? Would this organization really contact you this way? When in doubt — don't.

🚨 IF YOU'VE BEEN PHISHED — Emergency Response Steps
1
Change passwords immediately on all affected accounts
2
Activate MFA on all accounts if not already enabled
3
Contact your bank immediately if financial data was shared
4
Alert your IT/security team and escalate appropriately
5
Run a full antivirus and malware scan on your device
6
Monitor all accounts for unauthorized activity for 60+ days
MODULE 07

Real-World Case Studies

Famous phishing attacks that cost billions — and what we can learn

CASE 01 — 2013–2015 $100M+ Lost

The Google & Facebook Fraud

Lithuanian hacker Evaldas Rimasauskas created a fake company impersonating Quanta Computer — a legitimate hardware supplier used by both Google and Facebook. He sent fraudulent invoices via spear-phishing emails that looked completely legitimate. Both tech giants wired over $100 million combined before the fraud was detected. Rimasauskas was extradited and sentenced to 5 years in US federal prison.

💡 Lesson: Even Silicon Valley's most sophisticated organizations are vulnerable. Always verify financial requests through secondary channels — a phone call can save millions.
CASE 02 — 2020 130 Accounts Hijacked

The Twitter Bitcoin Hack

Attackers used phone spear-phishing (vishing) to impersonate Twitter IT staff and convince employees to hand over their admin credentials. Using internal tools, they hijacked 130 high-profile verified accounts including Barack Obama, Joe Biden, Elon Musk, Apple, and Uber — posting Bitcoin scam messages that defrauded followers of over $120,000 in 24 hours. Three individuals were arrested, including a 17-year-old.

💡 Lesson: Internal employees are prime targets. Voice-based social engineering is devastatingly effective. Zero-trust access controls and callback verification are essential.
CASE 03 — 2020 600% Attack Increase

COVID-19 Phishing Pandemic

As the world went into lockdown, phishing attacks surged by 600%. Attackers impersonated the WHO, CDC, government tax agencies, and vaccine distribution authorities. Fake "stimulus check" emails harvested banking credentials. Fraudulent "PPE supplier" sites stole payment data. Fake vaccine sign-up forms collected identity documents. Millions fell victim, leveraging fear and information hunger during an unprecedented global crisis.

💡 Lesson: Attackers exploit fear and uncertainty. During global events, misinformation spreads rapidly. Verify crisis-related communications through only official government websites.
CASE 04 — 2013–2016 $3.1 Billion Stolen

Business Email Compromise (BEC) Wave

The FBI documented a global wave of Business Email Compromise attacks targeting companies across 79 countries. Attackers spoofed or compromised CEO email accounts, then sent urgent wire transfer requests to CFOs or accountants. Instructions directed to "new vendor bank accounts" — actually attacker-controlled. Over 17,000 organizations were victimized. The FBI calls BEC the single most financially damaging cybercrime category globally.

💡 Lesson: Any financial wire transfer request must be verbally verified via a known phone number — regardless of how legitimate the email appears.
